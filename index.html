Revisiting the Fundamentals of Swarm Robotics: Event-based Analysis of Biological Swarms

Abstract

Scalability and high-speed coordination remain primary challenges in aerial swarm robotics even though the agility of individual autonomous aerial robots has increased dramatically in the recent years. To tackle these challenges, we revisit some of the core methods and assumptions commonly applied in swarm robotics by studying the underlying principles of the biological systems that inspire and motivate most of the research in this field. Specifically, we analyze maneuvers of large-scale flocks of Columba livia (rock dove) using data captured by an event stereo camera. This setup provides novel insights over existing research relying on standard cameras or Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS) trackers, such as 3D trajectory reconstruction with a microsecond-accurate temporal resolution or even wingbeat frequency and other visual cues that may be utilized by the animals for agile coordination. Our analysis shows that these biological flocks utilize a dynamic leadership mechanism driven by local visual perception, which challenges the classical consensus-based robotic approaches such as boids-like swarming. As we show in simulated experiments, a dynamic leadership-based swarming rule not only better explains the observed biological data when compared to a conventional approach, but also leads to more efficient trajectories of the individual agents. We publish details about our methodology including the design of the event stereo camera device for capturing the data, open-source implementation, as well as the datasets, to stimulate further research of biological swarms and the transfer of these findings to robotic systems.
